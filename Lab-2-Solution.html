Problem 1¶

We are going to imagine that we are looking for gamma-ray sources. In this kind of telescope there is a background of cosmic rays that provides a discrete noise term across the sky that precisely follows a Poisson distribution. To detect a gamma ray source, you need to ask what is the probability that the cosmic ray background would have given you a measurement as signal-like or more than the signal you receive.

To set up this problem, we will assume that the average cosmic ray background in one day is 1, and that the average number of gamma rays emitted by our hypothetical source is 2.

We found that the larger the number of days we averaged, the closer we got to a normal distribution with a mean of 1. B) If you have to look for a signal in 10k pixels, what is the probability distribution of your background? (Clearly state the statistical question, then turn that into math using your background distribution from part 1 of this problem.)¶

We assume that our background has a gaussian distribution with mean = 0 and sd = 3. If this is the case, what is the significance of a CCD detection signal strength of 16.232 from 10,000 observations?

$$10000*\int_{16.232}^\inf \frac{1}{3\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{1}{2}(\frac{x}{3})^2}dx$$

This equation gives us the expected number of events with an intensity of 16.232 or greater in 10,000 measurements.

Problem 4¶

The statistical issue we were exploring in the previous problem is called a trials factor (sometimes known as a look-elsewhere effect). This is an important effect, as if you search through a million locations, you would expect to se ~1 one in a million event. However, it is also often over estimated how big an impact this makes on the sensitivity of a search. So in this part of the lab we are going to invert the problem.

A) Calculate the signal required for a 5-sigma detection in Version 1¶

We know that z-score is always normally distributed. Because we believe our signal is also normally distributed, we can simply use the standard z-score formula to find the x-value (signal) required to find 5-sigma.

$$z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$$$$z*\sigma+\mu=x$$$$x = 5*3+0=15$$

To find a 5 sigma detection, we must input a signal of 15

B) Calculate the signal required for a 5-sigma detection in Version 2¶

To find this, we use an iterative approach. We increase the value of our measurement until we exceed five sigma, then take the previous (<5 sigma) measurement, reduce our step size, and continue iterating until we reach seven significant figures. This gives us a reasonably precise estimation of the signal necessary to reach five sigma after 10,000 trials.

In [359]:
z = 0
signal = 15
step = 1
sigfigs = 0
trials = 10000
while sigfigs < 7:
    while z < 5:
        p = stats.norm.cdf(signal, loc=0, scale = x)
        p10 = trials*(1-p)
        z = stats.norm.ppf(1-p10)
        signal += step
    signal -= 2*step
    z=0
    step = step/10
    sigfigs+=1
signal+= 20*step
print("The signal required for a 5-sigma detection in version 2 is approximately: ", np.round(signal,5))

The signal required for a 5-sigma detection in version 2 is approximately:  19.65162
C) Discuss how much brighter the signal must be for discoverey if you have a trials factor of 10k. Looking at your probability distributions, explain why the sensitivity penalty due to a trials factor is so low.¶

The signal must only be about 4.65 units brighter for the higher trial factor. Based on how quickly our probability distributions fall off, I believe this is because detecting a signal of 19.65 is 10,000 times less likely than detecting a signal of 15 given our assumed distribution. In other words, the rarity of higher measurements increases so quickly that even with a very high number of trials, the value necessary to reach a 5-sigma detection increases very slowly.
